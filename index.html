Wolters Kluwer AU

Re-inventing accounting software for the tax professional

Strategic Vision UX Research Stakeholder Influence Design System

My Role

This was a strategic vision project rather than a delivery engagement — the goal was to define and prototype a future-state product direction to secure organisational alignment and investment. Sole designer on the project, working in close partnership with the VP of UX. I owned the full scope: from initial product audit and user research synthesis through to journey mapping, concept design, and a fully interactive Figma prototype. I presented the final vision directly to the product and strategy leadership team to secure organisational buy-in.

Background

CCH iFirm is a professional accounting platform used daily by tax and accounting professionals across Australia — but it's not a standalone product. It's part of Wolters Kluwer's global software ecosystem, built on a foundation of shared modules used across multiple regional instances worldwide. This meant any strategic vision for the Australian product had to account for global constraints: shared components, cross-regional dependencies, and a platform architecture that couldn't simply be redesigned in isolation. Despite its critical role in their work, the product had accumulated years of technical and design debt. Its interface had barely evolved while user expectations and workflow complexity had grown significantly. A design system refresh was underway, but the deeper problem was structural: the product wasn't built around how accountants actually work.

Strategic gap analysis: marketing, product and content opportunities identified in the audit

Vision audit: defining the north star direction and product vision before redesign began

Problem

Accountants are under constant time pressure, yet the software was actively slowing them down. Rather than enabling efficient, proactive work, it forced users into reactive, manual patterns:

  • Constant context switching: Simple tasks required jumping between multiple tools and manually copying and pasting data, work the software should have been doing automatically.
  • Reactive rather than proactive: The product gave users no foresight into their workload. Accountants were always catching up, never ahead.
  • No feedback loop: There was no organisational mechanism to capture, analyse, or act on user pain points — no shared research repository, no insight taxonomy, no process for turning feedback into product decisions. This made it impossible to prioritise improvements or build a coherent roadmap.
  • Fragmented strategy: Multiple regional product instances meant there was no shared vision for where the product was going, leaving the team unable to align on a path forward.

This combination of surface-level usability issues and deeper structural problems created a rare opportunity: rather than patching the existing product, we had the chance to step back and reimagine it from the ground up.

The existing CCH iFirm: fragmented navigation, inconsistent UI, and no coherent workflow structure

Legacy screen examples showing the depth of accumulated technical and design debt

Process

I began with a thorough audit of the existing product, systematically documenting inconsistencies, friction points, and usability failures. This gave us a clear-eyed view of the current state before any design work began.

The most critical insight from this audit was that the product's structure didn't reflect how accountants actually work. Before designing anything, we needed to map their real workflow. Over several weeks in Miro, I worked closely with the VP of UX to build a comprehensive, highly detailed journey map covering every step an accountant takes from day to day. This became the single most important artefact of the project and the foundation every subsequent design decision was built on.

The comprehensive accountant journey map, built in Miro over several weeks, covering every stage of the accounting workflow

With the journey map established, I moved into defining the navigational framework, ensuring the product's structure mirrored the natural flow of an accountant's work. I explored multiple structural approaches and conducted desk research across professional SaaS products before making the deliberate decision to push beyond the existing design system and define a future-state vision.

The single unified interface concept: four workflow areas connected by a common data layer

Navigational framework V4: the structural layout underpinning the redesigned product

Competitor and industry research: benchmarking professional SaaS products to inform navigation and layout decisions

Navigation pattern exploration across professional workflow tools

Solution

The final deliverable was a fully interactive Figma prototype representing a complete reimagining of CCH iFirm, built around the accountant's natural workflow rather than legacy product structure. It covered:

  • A proactive navigation framework structured around the steps accountants actually take, surfacing what needs attention rather than waiting for users to go looking for it
  • Automated workflow patterns that eliminated the manual, repetitive tasks users were doing themselves: copy-pasting, tool-switching, status checking
  • Logic flows for all core patterns covering onboarding, account management, help, e-commerce, and trial-to-subscribe, each grounded in the journey map
  • A future-state design vision that went beyond the existing component library to establish what the product and its design system could become

Annotated dashboard design: showing the navigation framework and workflow logic in context

Final dashboard UI: a proactive, data-rich view of the accountant's workday

Logic flows covering all core patterns: suite settings, new users, product management, e-store and more

The desired experience: end-to-end accounting work in one efficient, unified workflow

Full Figma prototype structure: all logic flows mapped and connected

Prototype layout overview: the complete scope of the interactive prototype

Prototype overview in Figma: showing the full breadth of screens and connections delivered

Impact

The outcome of this project wasn't a shipped product; it was organisational alignment. The presentation to product and strategy leadership was met with strong endorsement, directly resulting in the formation of a dedicated Global Platform Team. I was appointed as the lead designer for this team, tasked with building the vision for Wolters Kluwer's next generation of global accounting software. The concepts developed in this project became the foundational backbone for that initiative.

Wolters Kluwer AU

Exploring integrated AI tax agents

AI Product Design Discovery Workflow UX Innovation

My Role

Sole designer embedded in a cross-functional squad with a PM and development team. I brought the user research and design perspective to the innovation process, owning discovery, concept validation, and the end-to-end Figma prototype handed to developers as a build reference.

Background

Wolters Kluwer runs an annual Global Innovation Awards, a company-wide programme that brings together cross-functional teams to develop ideas that improve internal processes or enhance customer-facing experiences. This project was born out of that programme, developed by the Asia-Pacific tax and accounting team at the moment ChatGPT broke into mainstream awareness.

With AI suddenly everywhere, we immediately looked at what it could do for our customers' daily workflows. I was brought into the squad specifically for my user research and design expertise, joining a PM and development team to take the idea through the multiple stages of the Innovation Awards, with each stage requiring deeper analysis of the solution, its revenue potential, and the problems it solves.

Problem

Tax professionals face a specific and painful set of research challenges that compound under time pressure:

  • Information overload: The sheer volume of tax information makes it difficult and time-consuming to surface what's actually relevant.
  • Time constraints: Professionals rarely have the time needed for thorough, in-depth research, yet accuracy is non-negotiable.
  • Accuracy and compliance pressure: Every piece of information needs to be verified against current regulations. The stakes of getting it wrong are high.
  • Cumbersome research process: The process itself was inefficient, with professionals cobbling together answers from multiple sources rather than getting confident, well-cited answers in one place.

Rather than assuming these pain points were real, we validated them. We ran an in-platform survey about the perceived value of AI assistance, and a fake-door experiment to gauge genuine user interest before committing to building anything.

Validation

The fake-door experiment reached approximately 3,000 users and recorded a 5% click-through rate, more than double the 2–3% platform baseline, giving us strong signal that the concept resonated with real users and was worth developing further, and providing the evidence base needed to progress through the Innovation Awards stages.

Fake-door experiment results: flow analytics showing user reach and 5% completion rate

CCH CiteGenie lean canvas: problem/solution fit, market sizing, revenue streams and key metrics

Solution

With validated demand, we developed CiteGenie, an AI-powered research assistant designed to streamline how tax professionals access and act on information, drawing directly on Wolters Kluwer's comprehensive library of trusted tax and accounting content, enabling professionals to ask real questions and receive accurate, cited answers in seconds.

We built out the solution using a lean canvas to stress-test all angles: problem fit, revenue potential, differentiation, and risk. I then designed a fully interactive Figma prototype covering the end-to-end user flow, serving as both a stakeholder communication tool and a developer handoff reference. The dev team built a working demo directly from this prototype.

CiteGenie home screen: ask real questions, get in-depth answers, create actionable content

Prototype screens showing the full CiteGenie conversation and citation flow

Beyond the core integrated experience, we explored additional distribution opportunities, including integrations into Outlook, Microsoft Teams, and even competitor software. This leveraged Wolters Kluwer's strong content position in the tax and accounting market to reach professionals wherever they already work.

Impact

The project reached the global final of the Innovation Awards — a strong result for a concept that started as a hackathon idea, competing against submissions from across the entire organisation. The fake-door experiment alone demonstrated real market demand, and the working prototype built directly from my designs gave leadership a tangible, credible vision for AI within the platform. Although CiteGenie placed second and wasn't taken forward as a funded initiative, it had a meaningful internal impact: it shifted the conversation about what AI could genuinely do for customers and generated significant excitement about the platform's future direction.

Wolters Kluwer Global

The path to a first-ever global design system

Design Systems Component Library Global Scale 20+ Teams

My Role

One of two designers on the dedicated design system team, working alongside the design system lead. My specific remit covered component design, documentation, and cross-product validation — with the key differentiator that I was simultaneously working on real, live products. This meant I could test and refine components based on how they actually behaved in context, how they interacted with other components, and how they held up against the full complexity of a real workflow, rather than designing them in isolation.

Specific contributions included: designing reusable components, writing component documentation, conducting research and analysis across products, establishing which components and patterns to prioritise, attending cross-functional global workshops, and maintaining feedback loops with development, product, and design stakeholders across the organisation.

Background

Wolters Kluwer is a global leader in professional information and software, operating across numerous distinct market segments with a product portfolio significantly expanded through decades of strategic acquisitions. This history created a fragmented landscape: disparate user experiences, inconsistent brand representation, and no shared design language across dozens of products.

Before the design system: inconsistent UIs, poor brand recognition and no shared design language across the product portfolio

The initiative to establish the organisation's first-ever comprehensive design system was a direct response to this fragmentation, a fundamental effort to unify products under a cohesive visual language and interaction framework across the organisation.

Pattern design: the next evolution of the system beyond components and pages, establishing reusable interaction patterns to bring consistency to complex workflows

Problem

With no shared design system in place, every team was solving the same problems independently, creating redundant work, inconsistent experiences for users, and a growing gap between design and development. Achieving consistency across 20+ teams and a diverse product portfolio required more than a component library. It required a strategic foundation, a shared language, and a system that could flex across genuinely different product types.

Process

We began with a comprehensive audit of existing Wolters Kluwer applications, meticulously documenting patterns, components, and pages across the portfolio to identify commonalities and inconsistencies. A key early finding was the distinction between two fundamental product types: Research products (horizontal fixed navigation) and Workflow products (expandable left-hand fluid navigation). This distinction became the structural backbone of the system's component architecture.

Chrome Fixed: horizontal top navigation for research-type products

Chrome Fluid: expandable left-hand navigation for workflow-type products

Through extensive cross-functional global workshops, we defined scope, established priorities, and navigated existing brand guidelines for icons, colours, and typography. Components were then designed and developed in agile sprint cycles, launching Version 1, iterating to Version 2, and progressing toward Version 3. Our toolchain evolved alongside the system, moving from Sketch to Figma for design and from Storybook to ZeroHeight for documentation, with all platforms linked and synchronised.

Design system fundamentals: buttons, typography, iconography and colour tokens

Storybook component documentation: components with V3 readiness status tracked across the library

Throughout, I worked across both the design system team and live product teams simultaneously, a deliberate approach that ensured components were stress-tested in real workflows rather than theoretical ones. This meant catching edge cases, interaction conflicts, and visual inconsistencies that would only surface when components were used together at scale.

ZeroHeight documentation site: the Product Design System introduction, vision and methodology

Impact

The design system is now an indispensable tool across the organisation. Before it existed, designers were solving the same component problems independently across every product team — wasting significant time and producing inconsistent results. Now, designers access and integrate components directly in Figma; developers implement the corresponding React and Angular code. This has dramatically accelerated design and development workflows across 20+ teams globally, freeing time previously spent on redundant component work to be redirected toward meaningful, user-centric problem solving.

Wolters Kluwer AU

Enabling efficient tax, accounting & legal research

Platform Migration UX Research Product Strategy 50,000+ Users

My Role

Sole UX designer across the full lifecycle of this project, from initial research strategy and contextual inquiry through to product vision, migration strategy, design system implementation, and launch. I led all research planning, scripting, analysis and synthesis, established a living research taxonomy in Dovetail, designed the end-to-end product-led transition strategy, and managed a junior UX designer hired to handle production design and developer collaboration as the project scaled. I also presented our discovery and design approach directly to executives and the broader organisation.

Background

IntelliConnect was a legacy legal and tax research platform with over 50,000 users across the APAC region. Loved by many, particularly long-term users who had worked with it for a decade or more, but built without design or user experience in mind, accumulating significant technical debt over the years. A global company directive set a hard deadline: sunset the platform by end of 2023.

The challenge was compounded by the fact that Wolters Kluwer had four products in market across the region: IntelliConnect, iKnow (for small accounting firms), a separate New Zealand product, and Pinpoint (for smaller legal firms). Smaller firms loved the newer platforms; larger firms hadn't moved. With a small APAC product team and a single UX designer, we had to consolidate all four into one future-proof platform without losing customers or revenue in the process.

This became Project Tahi, named after the Māori word for "one". The goal: one platform, one experience, built around how our customers actually work.

Research

First, we needed to understand the real pain points rather than rely on assumptions. In early 2020, with COVID having just hit and an 8-hour time difference between me (based in the Netherlands) and our APAC customers, we conducted all research remotely. By combining efforts across product, editorial, and commercial teams, we recruited and interviewed 10+ customers across Australia and New Zealand, including IntelliConnect users and those who had already moved to iKnow or Pinpoint.

Browse and search insights canvas: synthesising hundreds of data points into themed job-to-be-done clusters

I created the research plan and scripts, analysed all interviews, and ran the contextual inquiry process, synthesising findings into an affinity wall that anchored every subsequent design decision. The research focused on two core user pathways: search and browse. Both emerged clearly as the most critical pain points to solve.

Full interactive prototype: all screens and flows connected across user journeys for developer handoff and stakeholder review

Following this, I organised a full-day workshop to walk the wall, brainstorm design directions, and validate early concepts. We then returned to customers for moderated online interviews and unmoderated usability testing via Maze, using a high-fidelity Figma prototype to compare the efficiency of the new design against the old.

Browse and search usability testing: 29-slide Maze report comparing iKnow vs Pinpoint across all key tasks

In parallel, I built a living research taxonomy in Dovetail: a searchable, tagged repository of every customer insight across all research efforts, NPS scores, support emails, surveys, and satisfaction data. Any stakeholder could query it by user role, organisation size, region, sentiment, and more. It's still being actively added to today.

Dovetail research taxonomy: a searchable, tagged repository of all customer insights, filterable by role, org size, region, sentiment and more

Design

With research synthesis complete, I moved into designing the new platform end-to-end, from information architecture and navigation through to every core pattern and template. The Figma project alone spanned MVP, MMP, and MDP phases, with pattern pages covering every surface from search and browse to onboarding, history, favourites, and migration flows.

CCH iKnowConnect beta: the redesigned platform on desktop and tablet, showing the new home experience

User insights board: tracking and categorising iKnowConnect beta feedback in real time to inform iteration priorities

Migration Strategy

The biggest business risk wasn't the product itself; it was the transition. How do you move 50,000 users off a platform many had used for over a decade, without overwhelming customer success teams or triggering churn? The answer was a product-led transition, where most of the migration effort happened from within the products themselves, not through external comms or sales calls.

Figma project: MVP through MDP phases, covering every pattern page across the full iKnowConnect product

We designed a four-phase rollout:

  • Early Bird Beta: invite-only for high-value customers, giving us controlled feedback before broader release
  • General Beta: all IntelliConnect users could opt in to try the new platform, supported by in-product banners, onboarding flows, tooltips, and training
  • Automatic redirection: users redirected by default, with a temporary switch-back option for those still dependent on legacy features
  • Sunset: all four legacy platforms retired by end of 2023

Every transition touchpoint (banners, notifications, satisfaction prompts, onboarding flows, help files, content redirect mapping) was treated as a UX problem. All feedback collected during the beta was analysed and tracked in Dovetail, keeping the insights repository current throughout the transition.

Outcome

From product genesis in early 2022 to first beta launch in January 2023 to full IntelliConnect sunset by end of 2023. The new platform, CCH iKnowConnect, launched on time and delivered results that far exceeded expectations. Customers described it as "the perfect baby between iKnow and IntelliConnect", a product that combined the simplicity and modernity of the newer platforms with the depth and content richness that larger firms had always needed.
